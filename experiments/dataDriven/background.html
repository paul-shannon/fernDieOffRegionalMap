Extensive lab and field work and consultations with fern experts throughout the US lead us to conclude with considerable confidence:
  • The die-off is caused by an unidentified and possibly novel pathogen.
  • Climate change and local drought may be contributing factors, but until the proximal cause of the die-off is determined, the role of climate can not be known.
  • The die-off is unprecedented. We have no evidence that it is part of the P.munitum life cycle, in which ferns colonize open ground and rarely reproduce under a closed canopy, with individual plants typically living for hundreds of years. We tentatively claim that to a first approximation, and pending rigorous study of the population structures of healthy sword ferns in healthy forests, that sword ferns die of "natural causes" very infrequently.
  • The die-off has implications for the forest at Seward Park, and for other forests in the region.
